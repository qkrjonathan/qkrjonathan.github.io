Introduction

Back to top

At this point I've probably mentioned Exabeam, the Security Information and Event Management software (SIEM) I had the opportunity to test out and also present to the IT team during my internship, more than a few times in this blog. So it's become fairly clear to me, and most likely you, dear reader, that it's high time that I start properly committing to making SIEMs and log analysis and alert remediation etc. etc. become my thing.

How I got from there to deciding to pursue the Splunk Core Certified User certification is honestly pure chance. This word, "Splunk," had been planted into my head from having seen it constantly in the qualifications sections of job postings. So when my YouTube algorithm threw a little video about Splunk to see what would happen, like a grandmother in Central Park tossing breadcrumbs at pigeons, I happily took the bait.

Anyways, internet pipelines are a dangerous thing, and now I've committed to getting this certification and proving myself as a competent basic user of Splunk — and I assume to some extent most SIEMs.

Timeline


14 July 2025

Back to top

I have to give it to Tech with Jono with getting me started on this. His video on installing Splunk and downloading practice data for Splunk was that video that YouTube fed to me to see if I was interested. And indeed I was.

While I was setting up my homelab, I'd come across the idea a few times of getting a SIEM onto that homelab and learning how it works. But the lingering question in my mind had been, How do you practice log analysis and alert triaging or whatever on a SIEM when your network has literally nothing going on? The concept of practice data didn't even exist in my mind for some reason until this video.

To be clear, I wasn't following the actual instructions in that video step by step from the getgo. But it definitely sparked something.

That led to this video by CYBERWOX on how to pass the Splunk Core Certified User exam. This is how I found out such an exam existed.

Until this moment I'd had two distinct ideas in my head: (a) that I wanted to get into learning Splunk and having fun with it, and (b) I also wanted to have some kind of formal way of proving the experience I'd get along the way to put on my resume. Learning about the SCCU was when those ideas finally merged together.

Yet another source of affirmation was this video by TYTE about how she passed the exam after 10 days of studying. Now this was incredibly encouraging. Both of my certs so far (CCNA and Security+) took months. But two weeks?

Splunk's official test blueprint for the SCCU offers a "suggested and non-exhaustive" list of eight free training courses that cover the exam material:

  • Intro to Splunk
  • Using Fields
  • Scheduling Reports and Alerts
  • Visualizations
  • Working with Time
  • Statistical Processing
  • Leveraging Lookups and Subsearches
  • Search Optimization

I signed up for the Intro to Splunk course and got started. In the meantime, now following Tech with Jono's video from earlier (albeit using my Kali VM as the OS, not Windows as Jono did in the video), I downloaded the free trial of Splunk Enterprise as well as the Splunk Universal Forwarder. I got them as .deb files and unpackaged them with sudo dpkg -i [filename].

As it turns out — as it always turns out — my confidence from the first day always faces a little setback in the next. Having slept on it, and doing a bit more research, I got a bit more clarity on my roadmap.

The good news is that I learned two of the eight supposedly recommended courses by Splunk were paid, and they were not at all necessary to get SCCU certified. (See the above updated list.)

The bad news is that Splunk basically does not work on Kali Linux, and I spent all that extra time the day before trying to get it to work for nothing.

So, defeated, I returned to the video by Tech with Jono (who had done it properly on Windows the first time), and just redid the whole installation process on Windows. To no one's surprise, it worked perfectly.

Now, I could have done it on a Windows VM (and I think this is what Jono did); but instead I just set it up on my laptop. But when I was setting up my homelab and had to remake it from scratch, the truth is I didn't want to sit through the whole bootup process again when I didn't need it immediately (I only needed Kali and Metasploitable). Space limitations that would push me to use a VM aren't really an issue here (yet), as I'm on the Splunk Free license and I won't be ingesting so much data as to overwhelm my hard drive anyway — it's only one dataset I'm working with.

Once installation of both Splunk Enterprise, which I promptly converted to Splunk Free so as to not lose access after the free trial ends, and the Splunk Universal Forwarder is complete, I can verify that the logs from my host are being ingested into Splunk in two ways: first, opening Data Summary in the Search and Reporting homepage, then in a separate PowerShell instance running hostname and checking that the host shown under Data Summary > Hosts matches that returned by hostname.

Second, to see for ourselves the logs being ingested from the host, we run the search host=[hostname] in Splunk itself.

I also got the practice dataset, Boss of the SOC (BOTS) Dataset Version 3, that Tech with Jono had recommended, and once that was installed I ran the search index=botsv3 in Splunk to verify the logs had been ingested properly.

Here's a cool little visual I made showing the change in my laptop's disk space (as a percent of total) over the past couple days. 



                                
index="main" earliest=-2d@d latest=@h sourcetype="Perfmon:Free Disk Space" counter="% Free Space"
| rename Value as %_Disk_Space
| table _time, %_Disk_Space

This is combining a lot of what I've learned so far: the use of earliest and latest to set time ranges overriding the range picker, the use of rename, and the creation of table which Splunk then autoconverts into a line chart visualization. It also took a bit of digging around the ingested logs from my laptop; while by itself this isn't too fascinating I thought it was something fun to look at.

18-20 July 2025: Major homelab changes

Back to top

For unrelated reasons (another story for another post), I became fed up with my Windows 11 environment and did a complete overhaul of my homelab.

For one, I switched to Fedora as my daily driver, which has been incredible; it's minimal, it's a fresh change of pace, it's customizable, and it stays out of the damn way. It took a bit to get used to how Fedora works, with its .rpm packages and the default-hidden bottom bar. But overall it's been great.

Anyways, in that spirit I also switched my Splunk setup to the Fedora-based Red Hat Enterprise (RHEL), in a VirtualBox VM. In effect it only really changed the installation experience, and using Splunk was obviously the same. But overall switching to Linux again came with a great sense of relief. And thankfully, unlike with Kali, RHEL had no problem running Splunk whatsoever.

25-29 July 2025

(I had personal matters to take care of through the 24th)

Finally finished the courses, so I decided to take some time to review. I'd go in and quickly redo the quizzes after each course, and I'd also mess around with the BOTSv3 dataset to test out some of the commands. But this wasn't really intense studying, I admit ... maybe one or two hours in a day?

30 July 2025: Taking the test

Back to top

... And I passed! Checking in was a little nerve-wracking, because my camera would apparently keep freezing while the proctor made me show her my setup through my laptop webcam. But in the end it ended up working out, and I breezed through the exam — so much so that I became certain halfway through that I'd definitely overprepared.

But was it worth it?

Back to top

I guess it depends on how I look at it. In the sixteen days (total, though I certainly wasn't working on this that entire time) it took to get the cert, it was gratifying to feel a sense of purpose in this unofficial "gap-year" period where I'm still looking for a job.

Career-wise, it would obviously help most for positions where the company actually uses Splunk. But this comes with a couple side effects: one, because Splunk is so expensive at the enterprise level, I am definitely limited in the companies I can apply for. Two, Reddit will have you convinced that the SCCU cert is either a waste of money because you could just jump straight to Power User or Architect or something higher.

And that does make sense. But I still maintain there is some worth to getting this cert. The test was a motivation for me to actually hunker down and learn the basics of Splunk — and, to some extent, the core competencies for any SIEM — and I've come out on the other side feeling all the better for it. The test itself and its contents were easy enough that it was a much less intimidating introduction to the software than I had anticipated, which would not have been the case, I imagine, if I'd jumped straight to Power User following some Redditor's advice.

I'm rambling, but basically I'm someone who is looking to be a SOC Analyst, and this test is a tool to formally demonstrate my interest in that job description. Or at least, it's the start.

Next steps

Back to top

I think at this point, I should decide if I want to hone in further or broaden my horizons further. For the former, for example, I could work through the given questions included in the BOTSv3 dataset and have a blog post dedicated to a writeup for that. For the latter, I could move into malware analysis and work toward setting up my homelab in that direction.

Right now, my thoughts are: Why not both? There's definitely a way to do it. I'll figure it out in the days to come.
